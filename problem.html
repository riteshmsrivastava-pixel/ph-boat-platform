Section 01

The problem we picked, and why

The problem. The Philippines is an archipelago of 7,641 islands where the majority of the physical passenger fleet - 24,483 motorbancas5 versus 3,727 larger vessels in the last full pre-pandemic count - consists of small outrigger boats run overwhelmingly by single proprietors (81% of accredited entities4 in 2024). For tourists and institutions who depend on these boats, there is no reliable way to verify before boarding whether an operator is licensed, insured, seaworthy, or will show up at all. Trips are arranged by cash and walk-up at beach dispatch queues; capsizing of small bancas is the most fatal accident category6 in Philippine waters; and roughly 20 typhoons a year trigger Coast Guard no-sail orders that strand thousands7 with no refund or rebooking machinery.

Why verification is structurally impossible today, not just inconvenient. Executive Order 3052 devolved registration of boats under 3 gross tons to municipal LGUs. The consequence: there is no national registry of the small boats that actually carry tourists. The licenses, insurance requirements, and captain credentials exist in law (RA 9295, MARINA circulars) - but they are paper documents scattered across hundreds of municipal offices. The state created the quality signal and then made it unreadable. This is a textbook information asymmetry that no individual buyer can solve.

Why now. In April 2026, MARINA Memorandum Circular DS-2026-011 mandated online ticketing (SEAS/SOTS) for all scheduled passenger ships - RoPax and fast craft - while explicitly exempting motorbancas, recreational boats, and service boats. The state just digitized the big-ship segment and drew a regulatory boundary around the small-boat segment, leaving it as the last un-digitized, unverified layer of Philippine sea transport. Simultaneously, RA 11659 removed3 domestic shipping from the constitutional foreign-ownership cap, making the space investable.

Why we picked it over alternatives. It scores on every axis Prof. Suri's rubric weights: a genuine, evidenced market failure (not a convenience app); multiple course concepts applied load-bearingly rather than decoratively (information asymmetry, externalities, route-level monopoly, price discrimination, game theory); novelty (no team is likely to present a maritime market with a live 2026 regulatory shock); and it is interviewable this week - boatmen, tourists, hotels, and dispatchers are reachable by Zoom/WhatsApp through Palawan tour operators and hospitality contacts.

One-line framingquality in Philippine small-boat transport is valuable, legally defined, and unverifiable - so high-value trades either don't happen or happen unsafely. A platform that makes the state's own quality signal legible can expand total surplus rather than merely taxing captive mobility.

Who pays - B2B vs. B2C, and why we chose B2C

The payer question is the whole business. The prior memo proposed a B2B model: sell a certified-boat network to hotels, who pay a subscription. We reject that as the primary model on two evidenced grounds. First, disintermediation - once we introduce a hotel to a reliable verified boatman, the hotel has every incentive to message him directly next season and cut our commission; a B2B matching service without escrow or pier access decays into a free lead-generation tool. Second, TAM - a hotel-paid model restricts the market to concierge-served guests of upscale resorts and ignores the larger, higher-willingness-to-pay pool of DIY tourists who book ₱8,000 - 38,000 charters straight from their phones.

So the payer is the tourist (B2C), and hotels are kept as an affiliate channel that earns a commission for referring guests - not the primary payer. That is the hybrid this pack argues for. The one-shot nature of tourists is a feature, not a bug: they rarely repeat, so demand-side leakage is near zero by construction, and the whole retention problem lives on the supply side, where escrow and platform-only insurance solve it. The full five-model comparison, and the honest limits of the evidence behind it, are laid out in Research & Data § 2.8.

Honest statusthis B2B-vs-B2C verdict is currently argued from marketplace theory and TAM logic, not Philippine hotel evidence. The discriminating question - would a resort pay monthly for a certified network, or only refer guests for a commission? - is exactly what the Track C interviews exist to settle. We present the hybrid as a hypothesis, not a conclusion.
