Supply-side implication. Fragmentation is extreme (marketplace-favorable) but shrinking: the hull phase-out is a capital shock single proprietors cannot finance, so consolidation risk is real. A platform that channels prepaid demand - and potentially boat financing - to compliant small operators is aligned with the regulator, not against it.

2.5 · Regulatory facts (all sourced)

  • MC DS-2026-01 (Apr 2026), "SEAS/SOTS": mandatory online ticketing + e-manifests for all RoPax and fast craft; pilot Q2 2026, rollout Q3; 52 major ports within 2 years, all ports within 4. Motorbancas, recreational and service boats exempt. Compliance possible via own system, third party, or SEAS subscription (100% Annual Tonnage Fee discount up to 3 yrs for early adopters).
  • RA 11659 (2022): domestic shipping removed from "public utility" definition → up to 100% foreign ownership permitted.
  • RA 9295 + IRR: CPC regime, compulsory passenger insurance, and - Section 13 - explicit rate regulation on monopolized routes, i.e., the state legally recognizes route-level monopoly in this market.
  • Common carrier doctrine: "extraordinary diligence" standard; a platform that prices, packages, and takes payment risks reclassification as a carrier - liability design is existential, not cosmetic.
  • El Nido LGU tour regulation: the municipality mandates uniform tour menus and identical prices across all operators for the standard A/B/C/D tours, licenses guides, and caps daily visitors at sites like Big Lagoon. Price competition on standardized tours is legally shut off; competition occurs on quality, and charter/expedition pricing remains free.

2.7 · Failure precedents

Biyaheroes (PH bus/ferry aggregator, ₱50 or 10% service fee) failed to scale on low-margin domestic transit - direct evidence that thin-fare aggregation dies. Tripsta (OTA) collapsed on liquidity - evidence that holding customer money without weather-shock reserves is fatal in this geography. Both failures point the surviving design toward high-AOV transactions and escrow with disruption reserves.

2.8 · Model selection - B2B vs. B2C (the memo disagreement)

The two research memos split on who pays. Evaluating five candidate models against payer, addressable pool, and disintermediation risk isolates the hybrid - tourists pay, hotels refer - as the only structure that aligns high willingness-to-pay with the fragmented, SEAS-exempt supply. The B2B hotel model the first memo favored is rejected on incentive grounds, not distaste.

Hybrid: B2C marketplace + B2B affiliateTourists pay; resorts referUnregulated premium charter poolLow (escrow + SLAs)Recommended - high WTP meets fragmented supply
Reading this honestly. The verdicts above are argued from marketplace theory and TAM math, not Philippine hotel evidence - the disintermediation case rests on generic marketplace sources, and no resort, boatman, or dispatcher has yet been asked what they would actually pay. The single question that resolves B2B vs. B2C - "would you pay a monthly fee, or only refer guests for a commission?" - is carried into Interview Guide, Track C, with a matching Track B question on whether boatmen accept a 15 - 20% take with escrow. Until those return, the hybrid is our working hypothesis, not a finding.
