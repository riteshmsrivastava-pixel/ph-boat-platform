Section 01

The problem we picked, and why

The problem. The Philippines is an archipelago of 7,641 islands where the majority of the physical passenger fleet — 24,483 motorbancas versus 3,727 larger vessels in the last full pre-pandemic count — consists of small outrigger boats run overwhelmingly by single proprietors (81% of accredited entities in 2024). For tourists and institutions who depend on these boats, there is no reliable way to verify before boarding whether an operator is licensed, insured, seaworthy, or will show up at all. Trips are arranged by cash and walk-up at beach dispatch queues; capsizing of small bancas is the most fatal accident category in Philippine waters; and roughly 20 typhoons a year trigger Coast Guard no-sail orders that strand thousands with no refund or rebooking machinery.

Why verification is structurally impossible today, not just inconvenient. Executive Order 305 devolved registration of boats under 3 gross tons to municipal LGUs. The consequence: there is no national registry of the small boats that actually carry tourists. The licenses, insurance requirements, and captain credentials exist in law (RA 9295, MARINA circulars) — but they are paper documents scattered across hundreds of municipal offices. The state created the quality signal and then made it unreadable. This is a textbook information asymmetry that no individual buyer can solve.

Why now. In April 2026, MARINA Memorandum Circular DS-2026-01 mandated online ticketing (SEAS/SOTS) for all scheduled passenger ships — RoPax and fast craft — while explicitly exempting motorbancas, recreational boats, and service boats. The state just digitized the big-ship segment and drew a regulatory boundary around the small-boat segment, leaving it as the last un-digitized, unverified layer of Philippine sea transport. Simultaneously, RA 11659 removed domestic shipping from the constitutional foreign-ownership cap, making the space investable.

Why we picked it over alternatives. It scores on every axis Prof. Suri's rubric weights: a genuine, evidenced market failure (not a convenience app); multiple course concepts applied load-bearingly rather than decoratively (information asymmetry, externalities, route-level monopoly, price discrimination, game theory); novelty (no team is likely to present a maritime market with a live 2026 regulatory shock); and it is interviewable this week — boatmen, tourists, hotels, and dispatchers are reachable by Zoom/WhatsApp through Palawan tour operators and hospitality contacts.

One-line framingquality in Philippine small-boat transport is valuable, legally defined, and unverifiable — so high-value trades either don't happen or happen unsafely. A platform that makes the state's own quality signal legible can expand total surplus rather than merely taxing captive mobility.

Who pays - B2B vs. B2C, and why we chose B2C

The payer question is the whole business. The prior memo proposed a B2B model: sell a certified-boat network to hotels, who pay a subscription. We reject that as the primary model on two evidenced grounds. First, disintermediation - once we introduce a hotel to a reliable verified boatman, the hotel has every incentive to message him directly next season and cut our commission; a B2B matching service without escrow or pier access decays into a free lead-generation tool. Second, TAM - a hotel-paid model restricts the market to concierge-served guests of upscale resorts and ignores the larger, higher-willingness-to-pay pool of DIY tourists who book ₱8,000 - 38,000 charters straight from their phones.

So the payer is the tourist (B2C), and hotels are kept as an affiliate channel that earns a commission for referring guests - not the primary payer. That is the hybrid this pack argues for. The one-shot nature of tourists is a feature, not a bug: they rarely repeat, so demand-side leakage is near zero by construction, and the whole retention problem lives on the supply side, where escrow and platform-only insurance solve it. The full five-model comparison, and the honest limits of the evidence behind it, are laid out in Research & Data § 2.8.

Honest statusthis B2B-vs-B2C verdict is currently argued from marketplace theory and TAM logic, not Philippine hotel evidence. The discriminating question - would a resort pay monthly for a certified network, or only refer guests for a commission? - is exactly what the Track C interviews exist to settle. We present the hybrid as a hypothesis, not a conclusion.

2.5 · Regulatory facts (all sourced)

  • MC DS-2026-01 (Apr 2026), "SEAS/SOTS": mandatory online ticketing + e-manifests for all RoPax and fast craft; pilot Q2 2026, rollout Q3; 52 major ports within 2 years, all ports within 4. Motorbancas, recreational and service boats exempt. Compliance possible via own system, third party, or SEAS subscription (100% Annual Tonnage Fee discount up to 3 yrs for early adopters).
  • RA 11659 (2022): domestic shipping removed from "public utility" definition → up to 100% foreign ownership permitted.
  • RA 9295 + IRR: CPC regime, compulsory passenger insurance, and — Section 13 — explicit rate regulation on monopolized routes, i.e., the state legally recognizes route-level monopoly in this market.
  • Common carrier doctrine: "extraordinary diligence" standard; a platform that prices, packages, and takes payment risks reclassification as a carrier — liability design is existential, not cosmetic.
  • El Nido LGU tour regulation: the municipality mandates uniform tour menus and identical prices across all operators for the standard A/B/C/D tours, licenses guides, and caps daily visitors at sites like Big Lagoon. Price competition on standardized tours is legally shut off; competition occurs on quality, and charter/expedition pricing remains free.

2.7 · Failure precedents

Biyaheroes (PH bus/ferry aggregator, ₱50 or 10% service fee) failed to scale on low-margin domestic transit — direct evidence that thin-fare aggregation dies. Tripsta (OTA) collapsed on liquidity — evidence that holding customer money without weather-shock reserves is fatal in this geography. Both failures point the surviving design toward high-AOV transactions and escrow with disruption reserves.

Interview guide

Four tracks, 20 minutes each. Every track has (a) an objective tied to a model decision, (b) questions, (c) the idea-killing answer stated in advance, and (d) the discriminator — the single answer that selects between business-model variants. Interviews are the arbiter of the B2B-vs-B2C question the two research memos disagreed on.

Track A · Tourist / recent visitor

Objective: validate pre-trip booking behavior and a price premium for verification + refund protection. Decides: take-rate ceiling and Model A viability.
  1. Walk me through the last boat trip or island tour you took in the Philippines (or SE Asia). How did you find and book the boat, and when — before the trip or on the ground?
  2. What did it cost? Did you compare options? What information did you wish you had?
  3. Did you check — or even think about — whether the boat was licensed or insured? What would have made you feel safe?
  4. Anything go wrong: delay, cancellation, weather, overcharge, no-show? What happened to your money?
  5. For a private charter around $150–300: would you pay 10–15% more for a verified operator, money held until the trip happens, and an automatic refund/rebooking if the Coast Guard cancels sailing? What about 20%?
  6. Would you book that on a website weeks ahead, or wait and arrange it at the hotel/beach? Why?
Idea-killing answer: "I just booked whatever at the beach, it was fine, everyone does it, and I wouldn't pay extra for paperwork."
Discriminator: if pre-trip online booking + premium WTP holds only for private charters but not group tours, Model A narrows to the charter wedge — confirming the price-ladder thesis.

Track B · Boatman / operator

Objective: test supply-side adoption of commission + escrow + verification. Decides: take rate, payout design, and whether the long tail is reachable.
  1. What boat(s) do you run, on what routes/tours, and what papers do you need to operate? Who checks them, and how often?
  2. In high season and low season: how many days a month does the boat go out, and how full is it? What does an idle day cost you?
  3. Where do your customers come from today — dispatcher queue, hotels, repeat guests, Facebook? What share is foreign tourists paying charter rates?
  4. If we brought you prepaid private-charter bookings from foreign tourists: what commission is acceptable — 10%, 15%, 20%? At what number do you walk away?
  5. Payment lands in your GCash after the trip completes (escrow). Acceptable? What would make it not acceptable?
  6. Would you upload your registration, captain's license, and insurance to be listed as "verified"? What if verified boats visibly get more bookings?
  7. The wooden-hull phase-out: what's your plan? Would financing tied to platform bookings interest you?
  8. If a guest you met through us calls you directly next season — what do you do?
Idea-killing answer: "I'm full in season, cash only, no commission, and the co-op wouldn't allow it anyway."
Discriminator: acceptance of ≥15% commission with escrow makes Model A's margin math live; acceptance of commission but rejection of escrow forces a redesign of the retention mechanism.

Track C · Hotel / resort / tour desk

Objective: resolve the pay-vs-refer question that splits memo v1 (B2B) from memo v2 (hybrid). This is the highest-stakes track.
  1. How do your guests get boats today — transfers, tours, charters? Who arranges it, and with which operators?
  2. Tell me about the last transfer that failed. What did it cost you — refunds, comps, the review?
  3. What do you check about a boat before putting a guest on it? Do you carry any liability if something happens?
  4. When your trusted boats are full or grounded, what do you do?
  5. Two versions of the same service — tell me which is real: (a) you pay ₱X,000/month for a certified network, guaranteed backup boats, insurance on every trip, and we handle disruptions; or (b) you pay nothing, refer guests to our platform, and earn 5–10% of every booking.
  6. Under (b): after we introduce you to three reliable verified boatmen — honestly, why would you keep booking through us instead of calling them directly?
  7. Would you sign anything exclusive or preferred? What SLA term actually matters: response time, backup boat, refund speed, insurance certificate?
Idea-killing answer: "We have two exclusive boats we trust, they're never full, and we'd never pay or send guests to a third-party site."
Discriminator: chooses (a) → memo v1's B2B model revives with real WTP evidence. Chooses (b) → hybrid confirmed. Chooses neither → hotels drop out of the model entirely and Model A stands alone.

Track D · Dispatcher / cooperative / LGU tourism office

Objective: map the political economy of pier access. Decides: partner-the-co-op vs. route-around design (the latter has a documented failure precedent).
  1. How does the queue/rotation work — who decides which boat gets which customers?
  2. What problems land on your desk: complaints, refunds, no-shows, safety? What tools do you have?
  3. Klook and the big tour companies already sell tours online. How does the co-op see them — threat, irrelevant, partner?
  4. If online bookings arrived pre-paid and were assigned through your rotation, with the co-op taking a coordination fee — interesting or insulting?
  5. What happened when outside operators or platforms tried to enter before? (Probe for Caticlan-type episodes.)
  6. What would the LGU require of a platform: accreditation, fees, data sharing on manifests?
Idea-killing answer: "Bookings go through the rotation, period. Anything else gets blocked at the pier — it's happened before."
Discriminator: openness to rotation-integrated bookings converts the gatekeeper into infrastructure; hard refusal means pilot corridors must be selected by co-op posture, not tourist volume.

Section 05

Economics concepts

Each concept below is load-bearing: remove it and a specific design choice in Section 6 loses its justification. Concepts marked with the course list (public good · factor of production · information asymmetry · externality · monopoly) hit Prof. Suri's named criteria directly.

Course concept · Information asymmetry

A lemons market the state accidentally guaranteed

Akerlof's mechanism, verbatim: buyers cannot distinguish safe boats from unsafe ones, so they will not pay a safety premium; sellers of safe boats (who bear real compliance costs — licenses, insurance, FRP hulls) cannot capture that premium and under-invest; quality exits the market. The Philippine twist is that the quality signal exists in law — CPC, captain's license, compulsory insurance — but EO 305's devolution of <3GT registration to hundreds of LGUs made it unreadable. The platform's product is signal restoration: verification converts a pooling equilibrium into a separating one, where certified boats visibly earn charter-rate demand. Adverse selection prediction to test in Track B: currently, do compliant and non-compliant boatmen earn the same rates?

Course concept · Externality

Safety and congestion externalities, one internalized by us, one by the LGU

An unverified operator imposes expected-fatality costs on passengers and reputational costs on the whole corridor (one capsizing headline suppresses demand for every boat in El Nido) — a negative externality individual operators don't price. Verification plus platform insurance internalizes it: the operator's compliance now directly determines his booking volume. Contrast with the LGU's own instrument for the congestion externality — Big Lagoon daily visitor caps — a quantity regulation (quota) we must operate within, not around. Positive externality on the flip side: each verified boat added increases the value of the network to every buyer (indirect network effects), the standard two-sided-market argument for why this market under-provides matching without a platform.

Course concept · Monopoly / market power

Route-level monopoly is written into Philippine law

Sea routes are natural monopolies at low density: one vessel's fixed cost against thin demand means duopoly is unprofitable. RA 9295's IRR Section 13 explicitly regulates rates on "monopolized routes" — the regulator concedes the market structure. Two further monopoly structures in our corridors: (i) beach cooperatives are labor-side monopolies over pier access (Caticlan coop blocking Montenegro Lines = entry deterrence in action); (ii) the El Nido LGU's uniform-price mandate on standard tours is a legal price-fixing regime — a state-run cartel price that eliminates Bertrand competition on standardized tours and shifts all competition to quality. Strategy consequence: on price-fixed group tours we have no lever; on unregulated charters, price is free and quality certification is the differentiator.

Course concept · Factor of production

Capital shock in the fishing-boat economy

The bangka is the boatman's entire capital stock. MC 2016-02's wooden-hull phase-out mandates replacing a depreciated wooden asset with a ₱5–8M fiberglass hull — a capital requirement of roughly 10–20× annual owner earnings, with no collateralizable cash-flow history because the business is cash-based. Classic credit rationing under asymmetric information (the boatman can't prove his income). Platform booking history is exactly the missing signal: verified revenue data makes the boat financeable. This is the second-stage wedge and it deliberately rhymes with Walmy's alternative-data-lending thesis — the platform generates the alternative data.

Course concept · Public good (and why we mostly don't claim it)

Manifests and safety data

Honest scoring, per our own earlier calibration: the core service is excludable and rival — not a public good. The defensible sliver: standardized e-manifests and incident data shared with the Coast Guard are non-rival safety information that improves rescue response for everyone (SEAS's own justification). We claim public-good characteristics for the data layer only, and say so — over-claiming here costs credibility with a grader who checks definitions.

Pricing · 2nd & 3rd degree discrimination, two-part tariffs

Charge the gradient the price ladder reveals

Third-degree: tourists vs. residents differ in elasticity by an order of magnitude (₱38,000 charters vs. ₱100 hops); we price the inelastic segment and exempt the elastic one — which is simultaneously the welfare/equity argument. Second-degree (versioning/menu): group seat → private outrigger → speedboat → multi-day expedition is a self-selection menu; buyers reveal WTP by choosing, no identification needed. Two-part tariff, held in reserve: if Track C answers "pay," hotels get subscription (access) + per-booking fee (usage) — the textbook structure when a buyer's demand is recurring and the marginal booking is cheap to serve.

Transaction costs & incomplete markets

Why the trade doesn't happen today

Coase: the tourist-boatman trade at charter rates fails on search costs (he's invisible online), bargaining costs (language, no posted prices), and enforcement costs (no recourse on no-show; no refund machinery on no-sail days). The market that's missing isn't boats — it's contracts: enforceable, weather-contingent, insured passage agreements. Escrow + standardized cancellation terms are the contract technology. The measurable welfare claim: trades with positive surplus (tourist WTP $200 > boatman WTA $120) that currently die on transaction costs now execute; the platform's take is bounded by the transaction cost it eliminates.

Game theory

Disintermediation as a repeated game; entry as a coordination game

Boatman-platform bypass: one-shot logic says defect (take the direct booking, save 15%). Escrow + platform-only insurance + booking-history-as-collateral convert it to a repeated game where defection forfeits the future stream (prepaid demand, financing eligibility) for a one-time 15% — cooperation becomes subgame-optimal without exclusivity contracts we couldn't enforce anyway. Hotel bypass (if Track C says "refer"): tourists are one-shot players, so the leak is small by construction. Co-op entry game: routing bookings through the rotation with a coordination fee changes the co-op's payoff from "blockade" (Caticlan equilibrium) to "toll collector" — we buy the gate rather than storm it. Incumbent response: Klook's rational reaction to a niche verification layer is acquisition or ignoring, not a price war on a segment they can't onboard.

6.3 · Disintermediation defense (the question that killed memo v1)

  • Demand side: tourists are one-shot; leakage ≈ 0 by construction. (The reason B2C beats hotel-paid B2B on this axis.)
  • Supply side: escrow (no upfront cash to steal), insurance valid only on-platform, contact masking pre-payment, and booking history as loan collateral — repeated-game payoffs favor staying.
  • Channel side: hotels as affiliates earn from referring; bypassing us costs them the commission and re-imposes the liability we absorb.

Title — The fleet the state cannot see

A verified marketplace for Philippine small-boat charters
  • Team, course, date; one image: bangka at El Nido pier
  • Verdict line: pivot tested against data twice; presenting a hypothesis + the interviews that will kill or confirm it
2

The problem is a market failure, not an inconvenience

Quality is legally defined, economically valuable, and unverifiable
  • EO 305: <3GT registration devolved to LGUs → no national registry
  • 4,467 accidents 2015–20; banca capsizing = most fatal category
  • ~20 typhoons/yr; 6,607 stranded in one storm; no refund machinery
3

Market definition — route-level, segment-level

Not "the ferry market": private small-boat transfers & charters in tourist corridors
  • Fig. 1 price ladder: ₱100 hop → ₱38,000 charter on the same asset class
  • El Nido 500K + Siargao 530K arrivals (2023)
  • Supply: 24,483 bancas; 81% single proprietorships
4

Why now — the regulator just drew our market boundary

SEAS (MC DS-2026-01) digitizes big ships and exempts bancas
  • Scheduled segment: state-mandated e-ticketing, Barkota entrenched → we exit
  • Exempt segment: last un-digitized layer, ours to verify
  • RA 11659: 100% foreign ownership now legal → investable
5

Competition — the gap is supply, not demand

OTAs sell curated charters; 2,785 single-proprietor boatmen are invisible
  • Map: Barkota/Tripket (scheduled) · Klook/GYG (curated tours, 20–35% take) · integrated locals (own boats) · beach cash (everything else)
  • Failure precedents: Biyaheroes (thin fares), Tripsta (no disruption reserves)
6

The model

Escrow, insurance, and weather refunds are the product; booking is the wrapper
  • Fig. 2 flow diagram; take 15–20% on charters only
  • Hotels = affiliates (dashed, pending interviews); co-op = paid coordination partner (dashed)
  • Agent-not-carrier liability architecture
7

Economics I — asymmetric information & externalities

A lemons market where the state built the signal and lost the reader
  • Akerlof separating equilibrium via verification
  • Safety externality internalized by insurance; congestion handled by LGU quotas (Big Lagoon caps)
  • Public-good claim limited honestly to the manifest/safety data layer
8

Economics II — monopoly, pricing, game theory

Route-level monopoly is in the statute; the LGU runs a legal cartel on tour prices
  • RA 9295 IRR §13 regulates monopolized-route rates; El Nido uniform-price mandate kills Bertrand on group tours → we monetize unregulated charters
  • 3rd-degree PD (tourists, not residents) + versioned menu (2nd-degree)
  • Disintermediation as repeated game: escrow + insurance + financing flip the payoffs
9

Unit economics — shown as a sensitivity, not a promise

13% contribution margin; breakeven 1,020–3,070 bookings/mo depending on OpEx
  • AOV $150–175 sourced from operator rate cards; take rate is a hypothesis Track B tests
  • Base case needs 3 corridors, not 1 — stated plainly
  • Welfare slide-note: surplus from trades transaction costs currently kill; zero take on resident mobility
